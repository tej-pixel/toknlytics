LLM pricing, explained

What you actually pay for when you call GPT-4o, Claude or Gemini — and how to pick the cheapest model that still does the job.

If your AI bill feels unpredictable, it's usually because two levers are hidden: which model you call, and how many tokens each call burns. Here's how the pricing works and where the money goes.

Everything is priced per token

A token is roughly ¾ of a word. Providers charge separately for the tokens you send (input) and the tokens the model writes back (output), quoted per million tokens. Output is almost always priced higher than input — often 3–5× — so chatty responses cost more than long prompts of the same size.

The price spread is enormous

The gap between a small model and a frontier model is not 2× — it can be 20–50× for the same number of tokens. A lightweight model might cost cents per million tokens while a top-tier model runs into tens of dollars. That means the single biggest cost decision you make is model selection per task, not the prompt.

Rule of thumb: use the smallest model that passes your quality bar for each task, and reserve the frontier model for the calls that genuinely need it. Routing is where most teams find their first big savings.

What drives your bill

  • Model choice — the 20–50× lever. Right-sizing here dwarfs everything else.
  • Output length — the priciest tokens. Cap and structure responses where you can.
  • Prompt bloat — oversized system prompts and context inflate the input side on every single call.
  • Repeat calls — duplicate and near-duplicate requests you could cache.
